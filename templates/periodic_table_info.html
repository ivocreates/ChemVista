Understanding the Periodic Table

A comprehensive guide to the organization and patterns of chemical elements

What is the Periodic Table?

The periodic table is a systematic arrangement of all known chemical elements, organized by their atomic number and exhibiting periodic trends in their properties.

Created by Dmitri Mendeleev in 1869, the modern periodic table contains 118 confirmed elements, from hydrogen (the lightest) to oganesson (the heaviest). This elegant organization reveals fundamental patterns in nature and allows scientists to predict the properties of elements and their compounds.

Quick Facts
  • 118 confirmed elements
  • 18 groups (columns)
  • 7 periods (rows)
  • 4 main blocks (s, p, d, f)

Element Categories

Alkali Metals

Highly reactive metals in Group 1. Soft, low melting points, react violently with water.

Examples: Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs, Fr
Alkaline Earth Metals

Reactive metals in Group 2. Harder than alkali metals, react with water at high temperatures.

Examples: Be, Mg, Ca, Sr, Ba, Ra
Transition Metals

Dense, hard metals with high melting points. Form colored compounds, multiple oxidation states.

Examples: Fe, Cu, Ag, Au, Pt, Zn
Post-transition Metals

Softer metals with lower melting points than transition metals. Include aluminum, tin, lead.

Examples: Al, Ga, In, Sn, Pb, Bi
Metalloids

Elements with properties between metals and nonmetals. Important in semiconductors.

Examples: B, Si, Ge, As, Sb, Te
Nonmetals

Poor conductors of heat and electricity. Essential for life processes.

Examples: H, C, N, O, P, S
Halogens

Highly reactive nonmetals in Group 17. Form salts when combined with metals.

Examples: F, Cl, Br, I, At
Noble Gases

Inert gases in Group 18. Stable electron configurations, rarely form compounds.

Examples: He, Ne, Ar, Kr, Xe, Rn

Periodic Trends

Atomic Radius

Across a period: Decreases (left to right)

Down a group: Increases (top to bottom)

As nuclear charge increases across a period, electrons are pulled closer. Down a group, additional electron shells increase the size.
Ionization Energy

Across a period: Increases (left to right)

Down a group: Decreases (top to bottom)

Energy required to remove an electron. Higher nuclear charge and smaller size make electrons harder to remove.
Electronegativity

Across a period: Increases (left to right)

Down a group: Decreases (top to bottom)

Tendency to attract electrons in a chemical bond. Fluorine is the most electronegative element.
Metallic Character

Across a period: Decreases (left to right)

Down a group: Increases (top to bottom)

Tendency to lose electrons and form positive ions. Metals are on the left, nonmetals on the right.

Historical Development

1869
Dmitri Mendeleev

Created the first widely accepted periodic table, organized by atomic weight. Left gaps for undiscovered elements and predicted their properties.

1913
Henry Moseley

Discovered that atomic number (number of protons) is the organizing principle, not atomic weight. This resolved inconsistencies in Mendeleev's table.

1940s
Glenn T. Seaborg

Synthesized and identified transuranium elements (beyond uranium). Proposed the actinide series concept.

Present
Modern Periodic Table

Contains 118 confirmed elements. Scientists continue to create superheavy elements in laboratories, extending the table's reach.
